        <div class="source-citation">President Jimmy Carter · September 18, 1978</div>

        <div class="source-excerpt">

          <div class="excerpt-label">Read This Excerpt</div>

          <p>When we first arrived at Camp David, the weights of history and the hatreds of the present bore down heavily on all of us. President Sadat and Prime Minister Begin had come with the knowledge that failure could mean continued conflict between their nations. Both understood that their people longed for stability, yet both faced pressures at home that made compromise difficult.</p>

          <p>The framework for peace agreed upon at Camp David establishes a basis for peace not only between Egypt and Israel, but also between Israel and each of its other neighbors. It recognizes that for peace to be lasting, it must involve recognition that the rights of the people who live in these lands must be respected and their requirements for a better life addressed.</p>

          <p>The Camp David agreement has three parts. First, it provides for the full exercise of Egyptian sovereignty over the Sinai Peninsula. Second, it outlines a process for establishing an elected self-governing authority in the West Bank and Gaza. Third, it calls for negotiations to determine the final status of these territories and their relationship with their neighbors.</p>

          <p>We have no illusions. We know that the road ahead is long and difficult. But we also know that the alternative to peace is continued suffering, continued distrust, and continued fear. No one gains from the absence of peace. We are determined that the momentum generated at Camp David will not be lost.</p>

          <div class="excerpt-label">Read This Excerpt</div>

          <p>During my first press conference as President, I pointed out that the Soviet leaders have openly and publicly declared that the only morality they recognize is that which will further their cause, which is world revolution. I think I should point out that I have always maintained that the struggle going on for the world will never be decided by bombs or rockets, by armies or military might. The real crisis we face today is a spiritual one; at root, it is a test of moral will and faith.</p>

          <p>While America's military strength is important, let me add here that I have always believed that the greatest of all strategies for peace is the idea of freedom. The source of our strength in the quest for human freedom is not material but spiritual.           <p>I urge you to beware the temptation of pride, the temptation of blithely declaring yourselves above it all and label both sides equally at fault, to ignore the facts of history and the aggressive impulses of an evil empire, to simply call the arms race a giant misunderstanding and thereby remove yourself from the struggle between right and wrong and good and evil.</p>

          <p>I believe that communism is another sad, bizarre chapter in human history whose last pages even now are being written. I believe this because the source of our strength in the quest for human freedom is not material, but spiritual. And because it knows no limitation, it must terrify and ultimately triumph over those who would enslave their fellow man.</p>

        <p class="source-text">Signed October 26, 2001. Expanded surveillance authority, "sneak and peek" searches, and access to records.</p>

        <div class="source-excerpt">

          <div class="excerpt-label">Read This Excerpt (Simplified Summary of Key Provisions)</div>

          <p><strong>Title II: Enhanced Surveillance Procedures.</strong> The PATRIOT Act gave federal law enforcement agencies expanded authority to gather intelligence within the United States. It allowed the government to conduct "roving wiretaps" that follow a person rather than a single phone line. It also permitted the FBI to obtain court orders requiring businesses, libraries, and internet providers to turn over records about customers, including which books they checked out and which websites they visited, without informing the person being investigated.</p>

          <p><strong>Title V: "Sneak and Peek" Searches.</strong> Before the PATRIOT Act, law enforcement was generally required to notify the subject of a search warrant before or at the time of the search. The Act allowed delayed notification, meaning agents could search a home or office and wait days, weeks, or even months before telling the person. Supporters argued this prevented suspects from destroying evidence. Critics said it violated the Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches.</p>

          <p><strong>Title VIII: Expanded Definition of Terrorism.</strong> The Act broadened the legal definition of "domestic terrorism" to include activities that "appear to be intended to influence the policy of a government by intimidation or coercion." Civil liberties organizations warned this language was vague enough to apply to political protesters, environmental activists, or religious groups, not just violent extremists.</p>

          <p><strong>The Debate.</strong> The PATRIOT Act passed the Senate 98-1 and the House 357-66, with strong bipartisan support in the weeks after September 11. Supporters argued it gave law enforcement the tools they needed to prevent another attack. Opponents, including the ACLU and some members of Congress, argued it traded fundamental constitutional rights for a false sense of security. Senator Russ Feingold, the only senator to vote no, warned: "Preserving our freedom is one of the main reasons we are now combating international terrorism."</p>
