Unit Overview

This unit runs approximately 24 class days across three phases. All assignment instructions and due dates are posted on Canvas. Your final artifact is submitted to the Canvas discussion forum so the whole class can read each other's work.

Phase 1
Name the Problem
Before you read history, you define the terms. What do peace and force actually mean? Who gets to decide? These questions are yours to answer first.
Phase 2
Encounter the Record
You read what presidents said and did — from Carter through the rise of terrorism. But you also ask: who was left out of these documents? What voices are missing?
Phase 3
Act
You build something — a podcast, magazine feature, timeline, or debate — that argues your position and puts it into the world for your classmates to read and respond to.
Before You Begin

Before you read any presidential speeches or historical documents, locate yourself in this question. History is not neutral — and neither are we. Where you stand shapes what you see.

Write in your notebook: Where I Stand

Answer these questions honestly before you look at any sources. There are no right answers. You will return to this page at the end of the unit.

1. Think of a time — in your life, your community, or something you've witnessed — when someone used force (physical, legal, social) to create order or safety. Did it work? Who benefited? Who paid the price?

2. When you hear the word "peace," what does it look like? Where is it happening? Who is in the picture — and who is not?

3. Do you think the United States has the right — or the responsibility — to use military force to create peace in other countries? What makes you say that?

Return to this page. After you've read the sources and built your argument, come back and re-read what you wrote here. Has your thinking changed? In what ways? This reflection can become part of your final project.

Carter Administration
Address to Congress
Carter's Address on the Camp David Accords (1978)

"Israel has agreed, has committed themselves, that the legitimate rights of the Palestinian people will be recognized."

President Jimmy Carter · Address Before a Joint Session of Congress, September 18, 1978
Excerpt

The first of these documents is entitled, "A Framework for Peace in the Middle East Agreed at Camp David." It deals with a comprehensive settlement, comprehensive agreement, between Israel and all her neighbors, as well as the difficult question of the Palestinian people and the future of the West Bank and the Gaza area.

The agreement provides a basis for the resolution of issues involving the West Bank and Gaza during the next 5 years. It outlines a process of change which is in keeping with Arab hopes, while also carefully respecting Israel's vital security.

The Israeli military government over these areas will be withdrawn and will be replaced with a self-government of the Palestinians who live there. And Israel has committed that this government will have full autonomy. Prime Minister Begin said to me several times, not partial autonomy, but full autonomy.

Israeli forces will be withdrawn and redeployed into specified locations to protect Israel's security. The Palestinians will further participate in determining their own future through talks in which their own elected representatives, the inhabitants of the West Bank and Gaza, will negotiate with Egypt and Israel and Jordan to determine the final status of the West Bank and Gaza.

Israel has agreed, has committed themselves, that the legitimate rights of the Palestinian people will be recognized. After the signing of this framework last night, and during the negotiations concerning the establishment of the Palestinian self-government, no new Israeli settlements will be established in this area.

From Carter's Address Before a Joint Session of the Congress on the Camp David Meeting on the Middle East, September 18, 1978. Full text available at the American Presidency Project, UC Santa Barbara.
Guiding Questions
Carter says Palestinian rights "will be recognized." What does this promise mean — and who made it?
Egypt and Israel signed this framework. Palestinian leaders were not at the table. Does a peace agreement work if one affected party isn't present?

Reagan Administration
Speech Excerpt
Reagan's "Evil Empire" Speech (1983)

"They are the focus of evil in the modern world."

President Ronald Reagan · Remarks to the National Association of Evangelicals, March 8, 1983
Excerpt

During my first press conference as President, in answer to a direct question, I pointed out that, as good Marxist-Leninists, the Soviet leaders have openly and publicly declared that the only morality they recognize is that which will further their cause, which is world revolution.

I think I should point out I was only quoting Lenin, their guiding spirit, who said in 1920 that they repudiate all morality that proceeds from supernatural ideas — that's their name for religion — or ideas that are outside class conceptions.

While America's military strength is important, let me add here that I've always maintained that the struggle now going on for the world will never be decided by bombs or rockets, by armies or military might. The real crisis we face today is a spiritual one; at root, it is a test of moral will and faith.

But until they do, let us be aware that while they preach the supremacy of the state, declare its omnipotence over individual man, and predict its eventual domination of all peoples on the Earth, they are the focus of evil in the modern world.

The Western World can answer this challenge, he wrote, "but only provided that its faith in God and the freedom He enjoins is as great as communism's faith in Man."

From Reagan's Remarks at the Annual Convention of the National Association of Evangelicals, Orlando, Florida, March 8, 1983. Full text available at the Miller Center, University of Virginia.
Guiding Questions
What does calling the USSR an "evil empire" reveal about Reagan's ideology? Is this diplomacy, rhetoric, or a form of force?
Reagan says the real struggle "will never be decided by bombs or rockets." Does his foreign policy match that claim?

Reagan Administration
Presidential Speech
"Tear Down This Wall" — Reagan at the Brandenburg Gate (1987)

"Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!"

President Ronald Reagan · West Berlin, June 12, 1987
Excerpt

Behind me stands a wall that encircles the free sectors of this city, part of a vast system of barriers that divides the entire continent of Europe. From the Baltic, south, those barriers cut across Germany in a gash of barbed wire, concrete, dog runs, and guardtowers.

In the 1950's, Khrushchev predicted: "We will bury you." But in the West today, we see a free world that has achieved a level of prosperity and well-being unprecedented in all human history. In the Communist world, we see failure, technological backwardness, declining standards of health, even want of the most basic kind — too little food.

There is one sign the Soviets can make that would be unmistakable, that would advance dramatically the cause of freedom and peace. General Secretary Gorbachev, if you seek peace, if you seek prosperity for the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, if you seek liberalization: Come here to this gate!

Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate! Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!

The totalitarian world produces backwardness because it does such violence to the spirit, thwarting the human impulse to create, to enjoy, to worship. The totalitarian world finds even symbols of love and of worship an affront.

From Reagan's Remarks on East-West Relations at the Brandenburg Gate, West Berlin, June 12, 1987. Full text available at the Reagan Presidential Library.
Guiding Questions
Reagan spoke from the Western side of the wall. East Germans could not attend. Who was this speech actually for?
Is "tear down this wall" diplomacy, a demand, or a form of force? What is the difference?

Bush Administration
Presidential Address
Bush's Address Announcing Operation Desert Storm (1991)

"Tonight, the battle has been joined."

President George H.W. Bush · Address to the Nation, January 16, 1991
Excerpt

Five months ago, Saddam Hussein started this cruel war against Kuwait. Tonight, the battle has been joined. This military action, taken in accord with United Nations resolutions and with the consent of the United States Congress, follows months of constant and virtually endless diplomatic activity on the part of the United Nations, the United States, and many, many other countries.

Arab leaders sought what became known as an Arab solution, only to conclude that Saddam Hussein was unwilling to leave Kuwait. Others traveled to Baghdad in a variety of efforts to restore peace and justice. Our Secretary of State, James Baker, held an historic meeting in Geneva, only to be totally rebuffed. This past weekend, in a last-ditch effort, the Secretary-General of the United Nations went to the Middle East with peace in his heart — his second such mission. And he came back from Baghdad with no progress at all in getting Saddam Hussein to withdraw from Kuwait.

Now the 28 countries with forces in the Gulf area have exhausted all reasonable efforts to reach a peaceful resolution — have no choice but to drive Saddam from Kuwait by force. We will not fail.

As I report to you, air attacks are underway against military targets in Iraq. We are determined to knock out Saddam Hussein's nuclear bomb potential. We will also destroy his chemical weapons facilities. Much of Saddam's artillery and tanks will be destroyed. Our operations are designed to best protect the lives of all the coalition forces by targeting Saddam's vast military arsenal.

Our objectives are clear: Saddam Hussein's forces will leave Kuwait. The legitimate government of Kuwait will be restored to its rightful place, and Kuwait will once again be free. Iraq will eventually comply with all relevant United Nations resolutions, and then, when peace is restored, it is our hope that Iraq will live as a peaceful and cooperative member of the family of nations, thus enhancing the security and stability of the Gulf.

From Bush's Address to the Nation Announcing Allied Military Action in the Persian Gulf, January 16, 1991. Full text available at the American Presidency Project, UC Santa Barbara.
Guiding Questions
Bush emphasizes that 28 nations joined this coalition. Why does that matter to his argument?
Bush hopes Iraq will become "a peaceful and cooperative member of the family of nations." Did that happen? What does the answer tell you about force as a tool for peace?

Clinton Administration
Presidential Address
Clinton's Address on NATO Intervention in Kosovo (1999)

"We act to prevent a wider war, to defuse a powder keg at the heart of Europe."

President Bill Clinton · Address to the Nation, March 24, 1999
Excerpt

We act to protect thousands of innocent people in Kosovo from a mounting military offensive. We act to prevent a wider war, to defuse a powder keg at the heart of Europe that has exploded twice before in this century with catastrophic results.

Ending this tragedy is a moral imperative. It is also important to America's national interest. Take a look at this map. Kosovo is a small place, but it sits on a major fault line between Europe, Asia, and the Middle East, at the meeting place of Islam and both the Western and Orthodox branches of Christianity.

Sarajevo, the capital of neighboring Bosnia, is where World War I began. World War II and the Holocaust engulfed this region. In both wars, Europe was slow to recognize the dangers, and the United States waited even longer to enter the conflicts. After World War II, America led the effort to build the institutions and an Atlantic Alliance that have kept the peace in Europe for 50 years. Now we and our 18 NATO allies must help to protect the stability of that region.

I do not intend to put our troops in Kosovo to fight a war. Our troops will operate as part of a NATO force.

Do our interests in Kosovo justify the dangers to our Armed Forces? I've thought long and hard about that question. I am convinced that the dangers of acting are far outweighed by the dangers of not acting — dangers to defenseless people and to our national interests.

From Clinton's Address to the Nation on Airstrikes Against Serbian Targets in the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, March 24, 1999. Full text available at the American Presidency Project, UC Santa Barbara.
Guiding Questions
Clinton calls the intervention a "moral imperative." Who gave the U.S. and NATO the moral authority to make that call?
He says "I do not intend to put our troops in Kosovo to fight a war." What is the difference between airstrikes and war? Does that distinction hold up?

Clinton Administration
Memorial Remarks
Clinton's Remarks at the Oklahoma City Bombing Memorial (1995)

"The hurt you feel must not be allowed to turn into hate but instead into the search for justice."

President Bill Clinton · Oklahoma City, April 23, 1995
Excerpt

Today our Nation joins with you in grief. We mourn with you. We share your hope against hope that some may still survive. We thank all those who have worked so heroically to save lives and to solve this crime, those here in Oklahoma and those who are all across this great land and many who left their own lives to come here to work hand in hand with you.

This terrible sin took the lives of our American family: innocent children, in that building only because their parents were trying to be good parents as well as good workers; citizens in the building going about their daily business; and many there who served the rest of us, who worked to help the elderly and the disabled, who worked to support our farmers and our veterans, who worked to enforce our laws and to protect us.

And to all the members of the families here present who have suffered loss, though we share your grief, your pain is unimaginable, and we know that. We cannot undo it. That is God's work.

The anger you feel is valid, but you must not allow yourselves to be consumed by it. The hurt you feel must not be allowed to turn into hate but instead into the search for justice. The loss you feel must not paralyze your own lives.

When there is talk of hatred, let us stand up and talk against it. When there is talk of violence, let us stand up and talk against it. In the face of death, let us honor life. As St. Paul admonished us, let us not be overcome by evil but overcome evil with good.

From Clinton's Remarks at the Memorial Service for the Bombing Victims in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma, April 23, 1995. Full text available at the American Presidency Project, UC Santa Barbara.
Guiding Questions
Clinton addresses grief but warns against letting anger turn to hate. Who was he speaking to — and what was he worried about?
The bomber was a white American military veteran, not a foreign terrorist. How does that change how we read this speech and this event?

Rise of Terrorism
Federal Legislation
USA PATRIOT Act — Key Provisions (2001)

Signed October 26, 2001. Passed the Senate 98–1 and the House 357–66, weeks after the September 11 attacks.

Public Law 107-56, 107th Congress · October 26, 2001
Statutory Text — Three Key Provisions

Section 213 — Delayed Notification ("Sneak and Peek"): "With respect to the issuance of any warrant or court order under this section...to search for and seize any property or material that constitutes evidence of a criminal offense in violation of the laws of the United States, any notice required...may be delayed if — (1) the court finds reasonable cause to believe that providing immediate notification of the execution of the warrant may have an adverse result..."

Section 215 — Access to Business Records: "The Director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation or a designee of the Director (whose rank shall be no lower than Assistant Special Agent in Charge) may make an application for an order requiring the production of any tangible things (including books, records, papers, documents, and other items) for an investigation to protect against international terrorism..."

Section 802 — Definition of Domestic Terrorism: "...the term 'domestic terrorism' means activities that — (A) involve acts dangerous to human life that are a violation of the Federal criminal laws; (B) appear to be intended — (i) to intimidate or coerce a civilian population; (ii) to influence the policy of a government by intimidation or coercion; or (iii) to affect the conduct of a government by mass destruction, assassination, or kidnapping; and (C) occur primarily within the territorial jurisdiction of the United States."

From Public Law 107-56, USA PATRIOT Act, signed October 26, 2001. Full text available at congress.gov.
Guiding Questions
Section 215 allows the government to demand "books, records, papers, documents." What rights does this put in tension? Why did 98 senators vote yes?
Read Section 802's definition of domestic terrorism carefully. Could this language apply to protest? To civil disobedience? Who decides?

🚀 From Planner to Product

Your notebook is your blueprint. Every note, piece of evidence, and vocabulary word you recorded is a resource you can pull directly into your final project. Work through the steps below in order.

1
Review Your Claim & Refine It
Re-read your claim from Step 3. Is it specific? Does it answer "Can you create peace by force?" with nuance? Strengthen it before you start creating.
💡 A Level 4 claim includes a condition ("it depends on…") or acknowledges complexity. Add one sentence that shows you understand both sides.
2
Gather Your Best Evidence
Pull your strongest 3 pieces of evidence from your notebook. Be specific — names, dates, and outcomes. "The defense budget rose 40% between 1981 and 1985" is strong. "Reagan increased defense spending" is weak.
💡 Consider including a missing voice source you found. A missing voice used as evidence shows you went beyond the official record.
3
Apply Your Vocabulary
Choose at least 3 Tier 3 terms from your vocabulary notes (Step 4) and use them accurately in your product — not just dropped in, but in context.
💡 Define a term the first time you use it, then use it naturally afterward. This shows historical understanding.
4
Build Your Product in Your Chosen Format
Return to your format choice (Step 1) and its checklist. Follow the format-specific guidance to make sure your product meets all the requirements for your chosen type.
💡 Check the rubric (Step 8) as you build — don't wait until you're done.
5
Include a Visual or Map Source
Use at least one visual source from Step 2 (the StoryMap or the defense spending chart) in your final product. Describe what it shows and explain how it connects to your argument.
💡 Refer to it directly: "As shown in the Cold War spending chart…" or "The StoryMap reveals that U.S. forces intervened in the region multiple times…"
6

This rubric is based on the 9–12 Priority Standards for U.S. History. Use it to check your work before submitting.

How to use this rubric: Each row is a category graded 1–4. A score of 3 is proficient — it meets grade-level expectations. A score of 4 is advanced — it goes beyond by showing complexity, using primary/visual sources independently, or addressing counter-arguments. Your goal is to earn a 3 or 4 in every category.

Claim
4.1.B
 1No clear claim, or claim simply restates the question without taking a position. 2Claim takes a position but is vague or not historically grounded. 3Clear, arguable claim that directly answers the essential question and is distinguished from opposing views. 4Claim reflects complexity and historical insight, acknowledges conditions, nuance, or unintended consequences.
Evidence
3.1.A · 4.1.B
 1No specific evidence, or evidence is unrelated to the claim. 2Some evidence provided, but from only one area or lacking specific details. 3Evidence from at least 3 distinct administrations/events, cited with specific names, dates, and outcomes. 4Evidence is well-selected, includes primary or visual sources; anticipates opposing evidence.
Visual & Primary Sources
3.1.A · 3.1.B
 1No visual or primary sources used. 2A source is referenced but not analyzed. 3At least one visual or primary source is included, analyzed, and connected to the argument. 4Multiple sources (including at least one visual) are analyzed for author perspective, potential bias, and relevance.
Reasoning
4.1.B
 1No reasoning provided, evidence is listed without explanation. 2Some reasoning, but does not clearly connect evidence to the claim. 3Reasoning explains HOW and WHY each piece of evidence supports the claim; includes at least one consequence. 4Reasoning addresses intended AND unintended consequences; addresses a counter-argument.
Author's Perspective & Bias
3.2.B
 1No awareness of author perspective or bias. 2Briefly notes a source's author but does not evaluate how perspective affects conclusions. 3Evaluates at least one source for point of view and potential bias. 4Consistently evaluates multiple sources for bias and perspective to strengthen the argument.
Cause & Effect / Timeline
2.2.C
 1Events are listed without explaining how one led to another. 2Some cause-and-effect language used, but connections are general or unclear. 3Explains how multiple events are shaped by their time and place; shows how one event affected the next. 4Constructs a clear causal chain across administrations; evaluates how unique circumstances shaped outcomes.
Historical Vocabulary
4.1.B
 1No unit vocabulary used, or terms used incorrectly. 21–2 vocabulary terms used, but not always accurately or in context. 33 or more Tier 2/3 vocabulary terms used accurately and in context. 4Vocabulary is used fluently throughout; Tier 3 terms are defined on first use and woven naturally into the argument.
Format & Organization
4.1.B
 1Product is incomplete or difficult to follow; no clear structure. 2Some structure present, but argument is hard to follow or sections are missing. 3Product is organized, complete, and clearly communicates the argument in the chosen format. 4Format enhances the argument — structure, design, or delivery choices make the product compelling.
